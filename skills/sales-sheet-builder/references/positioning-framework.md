# Positioning Framework

Use this reference after building the JTBD map. A sales sheet is weak when it only explains the product. It becomes useful when it explains the buyer's current situation, why the current path is failing, and why this offer is a better way to do the same job.

In this skill, "competitor" means any current way the buyer tries to get the job done. That includes named vendors, internal workflows, services, agencies, spreadsheets, manual effort, hiring, doing nothing, or pushing harder on the existing process.

For startups and new categories, the chief competitor is usually the most likely status quo. Named vendors may be useful reference competitors, but do not center them unless the buyer is likely to compare or switch from them.

Competitor research should answer "which part of the job does this alternative help with?" and "when does this alternative become the primary way the buyer does the job?" A named product can be coopetition: competitive in one step, complementary in another, and only a budget competitor in a third.

## Four-Question Pitch Framework

Answer these four questions before writing the final sheet.

| Question | What to produce | Rules |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1. What are they trying to increase or decrease? | Name the target metric, risk, workload, cost, speed, quality, confidence, or outcome. | Be concrete. "Book more qualified meetings" is stronger than "grow revenue." |
| 2. What do they use today to move that number? | List tools, methods, services, teams, agencies, spreadsheets, manual work, AI tools, and workarounds. | Include status quo behavior, not just named software competitors. These are all competitors because they are how the buyer does the job now. |
| 3. What are the downsides of today's approach? | Name the buyer-felt tradeoffs: wasted time, weak fit, bad data, tool sprawl, trust loss, brand risk, manual cleanup, low visibility, high cost, slow cycle, poor quality. | Write this in the buyer's language. Avoid abstract strategy terms. Look specifically for deficiencies relative to the offer's advantages. |
| 4. How does this offer solve the same job without those downsides? | State the new mechanism, why it is different, the advantage it creates, and the proof available. | Do not claim "no downside." Name tradeoffs honestly if they exist. |

The final sales sheet should make the buyer think: "This understands what I am trying to get done, why my current path is frustrating, and why this approach might work better."

## Big Idea / Differentiator

The big idea is the compressed buyer-facing frame. It is not a tagline, feature list, or broad value prop. It is the sharp contrast between the buyer's current way and the new way.

Use this structure:

`If you are trying to [increase/decrease goal] by using [current approach], you inherit [core risk/problem/downside]. [Product] lets you [do the same job] through [new mechanism], reducing [downside impact] while also increasing/decreasing [additional outcome].`

The downside matters because it creates a concrete buyer impact. Do not force that impact into a fixed category. Derive it from the buyer's situation and state it in their language.

Shorter structure:

`Do [job] without [current approach downside].`

Stronger structure:

`Do [job] with less [old downside] and more/less [additional outcome].`

Message spine:

| Piece | What it says |
| --- | --- |
| The job | What the buyer is already trying to increase, decrease, avoid, or improve |
| The old way | The current approach, tool, service, workflow, workaround, or budget path |
| The old-way downside | The core risk, problem, waste, or tradeoff that makes the buyer open to change |
| Downside impact | The actual burden created by the old way, stated in the buyer's language |
| The new way | The product's mechanism for doing the same job differently |
| The reduced downside | What the new approach removes, reduces, avoids, or controls |
| The additional increase/decrease | The extra lift, reduction, quality gain, speed gain, risk reduction, confidence, visibility, or compounding advantage created by the new approach |
| The caveat | Any honest tradeoff, proof gap, or fit constraint |

Rules:

- The big idea should make the buyer feel seen before it explains the product.
- Anchor against the current approach, not just a named vendor.
- Use one dominant downside. Do not dilute the idea with five problems at once.
- Show why the downside is not merely inconvenient. Explain the real consequence it creates for this buyer in this situation.
- Connect the downside to the offer's specific mechanism.
- State the additional increase or decrease created by the new way. Without this, the idea only sounds like risk reduction.
- Avoid "better/faster/easier" unless you explain why.
- If the idea could describe three competitors, it is not differentiated enough.

Examples:

- `Book meetings with fewer bad-fit touches and more rep-ready context.`
- `Use AI for outbound prep with less brand risk and more human judgment at the moment of send.`
- `Find buyers from live signals with less list waste and more credible reasons to reach out.`

## Alternative Map

Research and classify alternatives into three buckets, then rank them by how likely they are to be the buyer's current path.

| Alternative type | Definition | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Status quo | What the buyer already does when they do not buy anything new. | This is usually the real competitor. It defines the pain and the switching argument. |
| Reference competitors | Named companies or products that buyers may directly compare because they appear to solve the same job. | These shape category language, expected features, and buyer comparison questions. |
| Economic competitors | Different ways the buyer could spend the same budget or effort to move the same number. | These shape the business case and why the budget should move here instead. |

Also group alternatives by value axis:

| Value axis | What the buyer values | What to test |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Access | More names, channels, data, or reachable buyers | Does access create quality or just more raw material? |
| Speed | Faster research, faster list building, faster send | Does speed reduce judgment or context? |
| Scale | More touches, more coverage, more automation | Does scale create trust or just activity? |
| Control | Human review, brand safety, governance | Does control slow the motion too much? |
| Context | Better reasons to reach out, more relevant prep | Is context repeatable and visible to leadership? |
| Cost | Lower headcount, lower tool spend, cheaper execution | Does lower cost lower quality or ownership? |
| Ownership | Relationship capital and learning stay inside the company | Does internal ownership require more operational discipline? |

Use the value axis to find the "reverse": buyers choose the alternative because they value something real, but that choice often creates a downside the new offer can reduce.

Examples of economic competitors:

- Hire another rep
- Use an outsourced agency
- Buy more data
- Add another sales engagement tool
- Spend more on ads
- Assign the work to RevOps
- Keep using spreadsheets and manual research
- Push more volume through the existing process

## Status Quo Priority

Before writing copy, choose the primary contrast.

Default to the status quo when:

- The product is in a startup, emerging, or newly named category.
- The buyer is more likely to use manual work, internal process, an agency, spreadsheets, a general-purpose tool, or "do nothing" than a purpose-built competitor.
- The direct competitors are interesting to investors or category analysts but not what most buyers actually use today.
- The buyer's switching decision is really "change the way we work" rather than "pick Vendor A over Vendor B."

Use direct/reference competitors as the primary contrast only when:

- The user provides a competitor as the known comparison.
- The source page names direct alternatives.
- Search evidence shows buyers commonly compare those tools for the same job.
- The buyer is likely already budgeted for that software category.

Rule: the final sales sheet should fight the buyer's most likely current behavior, not the most impressive competitor list.

## Deficiency-To-Advantage Map

After mapping alternatives, build this table. This is the bridge from research to differentiated copy.

| Current way they do the job | Why buyers use it | Deficiency or downside | Downside impact | Downside reduced by our approach | Additional increase/decrease our approach creates | Proof or support | Tradeoff to admit |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |

Rules:

- Compare against the way the buyer solves the job today, not against an abstract category.
- Let the offer, competitors, and most likely status quo reveal the downsides. Do not invent generic pain.
- Name the deficiency only if it is plausible, sourced, or strongly inferable from buyer behavior.
- Explain the concrete impact the deficiency creates for the buyer.
- Tie every deficiency to a corresponding product advantage.
- Separate downside reduction from additional upside. Both must be clear.
- Do not claim the offer is better at everything.
- Keep only the strongest 1-3 contrasts for the final sales sheet.
- Admit tradeoffs when needed. A credible sharp sheet beats an overclaimed one.
- Avoid using a fixed downside taxonomy. Examples like risk, tedium, embarrassment, inconsistency, or cost can help you think, but they are not the answer unless they match the buyer's real situation.

## Competitor Research Rules

- Use current web research for competitor identification because categories and positioning change.
- Start from the buyer job, not the vendor's category label.
- Search for alternatives using buyer phrases, category phrases, and "alternative to" queries.
- Read competitor homepages or product pages before naming them.
- Separate what competitors actually claim from your inference.
- Do not over-index on SEO listicles. Use them as discovery aids, then verify against primary competitor pages.
- Do not turn the sales sheet into a competitor teardown. The goal is a sharper buyer argument.

## Differentiator Thesis

Write one thesis before the final sheet:

`Unlike [status quo or alternative], [Product] helps [buyer] [do the job] by [unique mechanism], so they can [desired outcome] without [specific downside].`

Good thesis:

`Unlike buying another static prospect list, Audienti helps SDR leaders find prospects from live buyer signals, prepare reps with context, and review outreach before send, so the team can create more credible meetings without forcing reps to recover from weak names and generic sequences.`

Weak thesis:

`Audienti helps sales teams improve outbound with AI and better workflows.`

## Differentiation Checks

Before writing the final sheet, verify:

- The primary status quo is named.
- At least two reference competitors or competitor categories were considered when the category is competitive.
- At least two economic competitors were considered.
- The downside of the status quo is sharper than "inefficient."
- Each key alternative has a deficiency mapped to a corresponding offer advantage.
- The differentiator is tied to a mechanism, not a vague benefit.
- The product is compared against how the buyer solves the job today, not against a random vendor list.
- Any direct competitor claim is sourced.
- The final sheet does not make the product sound interchangeable with the alternatives.
