# Output Template And Quality Gates

Use this structure to produce two separate artifacts when the research is useful:

1. A compressed buyer-facing sales sheet.
2. A separate internal workbook.

Do not combine them unless the user explicitly asks for one combined document.

## Default Published Output

When the user asks for a sales sheet, one-pager, sell sheet, or buyer-ready copy, publish only the compressed sales sheet.

Use this exact heading:

## Buyer-Facing Sales Sheet

The research brief, JTBD map, alternative map, gap analysis, feature map, evidence log, proof gaps, and optional variants are internal scaffolding. Put them in a separate workbook when they are useful.

Only 10-20% of the internal workbook should appear in the sales sheet. The buyer should see the sharp conclusion, not the research machinery.

## Compression Rules

The sales sheet should cost the buyer as little mental effort as possible.

Rules:

- One clear buyer.
- One dominant job.
- One most likely current approach.
- One core downside.
- One new mechanism.
- One additional increase or decrease.
- No JTBD labels, strategy matrix labels, evidence-log language, or framework explanation.
- No raw competitor list unless the buyer needs it to understand the choice.
- No tables unless they reduce effort.
- No more than three proof points.
- No section that exists only to show the research.
- Prefer the fewest words that preserve the strongest argument.

The buyer-facing sheet should fit on one page or one screen-length brief when possible.

Write the sales sheet in this order:

1. Headline: buyer-level promise tied to the job they are trying to improve.
2. Subheadline: who it helps and what changes.
3. Category line: plain product category.
4. Current situation: how the buyer tries to solve the job today.
5. The cost of that approach: the real consequence created by the downside, in the buyer's language.
6. New way: how the offer solves the same job differently.
7. How it works: 3 steps only.
8. Proof: company proof first; if missing, use category or industry proof modestly.
9. Sales conversation hook: one simple prompt only if useful.

## Separate Workbook Output

When research, diagnosis, testing, tuning, or a durable evidence base is useful, create a second file with this name pattern:

`[slug]-sales-sheet-workbook.md`

The workbook is for the operator, not the buyer. It can be dense. It should preserve the useful data, alternatives, sources, and reasoning that made the sales sheet sharper.

When tuning from pasted feedback or a prior output, preserve the feedback trail in the workbook. Do not force it into the buyer-facing sheet.

### Big Idea / Differentiator

Use this structure:

`If the buyer is doing [job] with [current approach], they inherit [core downside]. [Product] lets them do [same job] through [new mechanism], reducing [downside impact] while increasing/decreasing [additional outcome].`

Also state the downside impact:

`That downside matters because [actual buyer consequence in the buyer's language].`

Then write the buyer-facing version in one line:

`Do [job] with less [current approach downside] and more/less [additional outcome].`

Default contrast:

Use the most likely status quo as the contrast unless evidence shows the buyer is more likely comparing direct vendors.

### Differentiator Thesis

Use this form:

`Unlike [status quo or alternative], [Product] helps [buyer] [do the job] by [unique mechanism], so they can [desired outcome] without [specific downside].`

## Plain-Language Rules

Use:

- Short sentences
- Plain words
- One idea per line when useful
- Specific buyer language
- Concrete examples
- Simple category labels
- Clear before-and-after contrast
- Active voice

Avoid:

- Jargon
- Internal product language
- Technical architecture
- Vague platform language
- Long feature lists
- Multiple buyer types in one sheet
- Claims the reader must decode
- Hype
- Generic claims
- Fake testimonials
- Fake case studies
- Unsupported metrics

Avoid these words and phrases unless the source proves the buyer uses them:

- Orchestration
- Activation
- Unlock

- Autonomous
- Agentic
- Platform of platforms
- Single pane of glass
- AI-powered growth engine
- Seamless

- Robust
- Powerful

## Rewrite Pass

After drafting, ask sentence by sentence:

- Would the target buyer understand this in five seconds?
- Does this help the buyer decide whether the product matters?
- Does this sentence explain pain, value, proof, category, or outcome?
- Is there a simpler way to say it?
- Does this sound like a person explaining the product?
- Does this sound like vendor copy?
- Does this add clarity or noise?

Remove any sentence that fails these checks.

## Buyer Effort Test

The sales sheet passes only if a buyer can scan it in 30 seconds and understand:

- What the product is
- Who it is for
- What pain it solves
- Why the pain matters now
- How the product works at a basic level
- What outcome they should expect
- Why this approach differs from common alternatives
- Whether it is worth a meeting

## Quality Checks

Before final output, verify:

- The sheet speaks to one clear buyer.
- The product category is clear.
- The buyer understands where the product fits in their ecosystem.
- The sheet names what the buyer is trying to increase or decrease.
- The sheet explains how the buyer solves the job today.
- The sheet names the downside of the current approach.
- The sheet explains how the offer solves the same job without the same downside.
- The status quo, reference competitors, and economic competitors were considered.
- The primary contrast is the buyer's most likely current way of doing the job.
- The main deficiencies of current alternatives are mapped to the offer's advantages.
- The sheet has a clear big idea that contrasts the current way with the new way.
- The sheet explains the concrete impact created by current downsides.
- The sheet names both the downside reduced and the additional increase or decrease created.
- The pain matches the buyer.
- The promise follows from the pain.
- The proof supports the promise.
- The differentiator is based on a mechanism, not a vague benefit.
- The product definition is simple.
- Benefits and outcomes outweigh features.
- Every number has a source.
- No company-specific result appears unless the source page proves it.
- The final sheet works without reading the original URL.
- The final sheet avoids jargon.
- The reader does not need technical knowledge.
- The sales conversation hook supports the sales goal.

The final output should make the buyer say: "I understand what this is. I understand why it matters. I understand whether it is worth a conversation."
