# JTBD Framework For Sales Sheets

Use this reference to diagnose the buyer's job before writing positioning or copy. This is a thinking tool, not a section to publish by default.

Do not add JTBD theory language, framework names, or matrix labels to the buyer-facing sales sheet unless the user explicitly asks for that. Use the framework to find the argument; publish the argument.

## Core Principle

Buyers do not start with a product category. They have a job they are trying to get done.

The status quo is the current way they perform that job. Competitors are the tools, services, people, workflows, and workarounds that occupy one or more steps of that job.

## Minimum JTBD Map

Create this before writing the sales sheet.

| Field | What to identify |
| --- | --- |
| Job executor | The person or team trying to get the job done |
| Core functional job | The main thing they are trying to accomplish, independent of any product |
| Increase/decrease goal | The target outcome, metric, burden, risk, speed, quality, confidence, or cost they want to improve |
| Current job steps | How they get the job done today |
| Friction points | Where the current approach creates delay, risk, burden, inconsistency, awkwardness, cost, lost quality, lost trust, or other buyer-defined downsides |
| Desired outcomes | What "better" means at each high-friction step |
| Related jobs | Adjacent jobs the buyer also needs done |
| Emotional/social jobs | How the buyer wants to feel or be perceived while doing the job |
| Consumption-chain jobs | What the buyer must do to buy, receive, install, set up, learn, integrate, maintain, repair, upgrade, or dispose of the solution |

## Core Job Steps

Use these common job steps as a discovery lens. Rename or adapt them to the buyer's real workflow.

| Step | What to ask |
| --- | --- |
| Define | How does the buyer define the target, scope, success criteria, or constraints? |
| Locate | How does the buyer find inputs, options, people, data, or opportunities? |
| Prepare | How does the buyer gather context, clean data, plan, or get ready to act? |
| Confirm | How does the buyer validate fit, timing, quality, risk, or readiness? |
| Execute | How does the buyer perform the main action? |
| Monitor | How does the buyer track whether the action is working? |
| Modify | How does the buyer adjust when reality changes? |
| Conclude | How does the buyer finish, report, hand off, learn, or decide what happens next? |

The friction is usually in the steps, not in the category label. Find the step where the current approach breaks, then write the sales sheet around that break.

## Outcome Statements

Write desired outcomes in plain buyer language:

`Increase/decrease [measure] when [doing job step] so [business or personal result].`

Examples:

- Decrease the time it takes to identify prospects worth a rep's attention.
- Increase confidence that a buyer is relevant before a rep reaches out.
- Decrease the awkwardness of asking for a meeting without a real reason.
- Increase consistency in how reps prepare for first touches.

Only use outcomes that matter to the buyer and are believable from the evidence.

## Competitor Buckets By Job Step

Do not list competitors as one flat pile. Bucket them by the job step or value axis they primarily serve.

| Bucket | What buyers value | Common downside to investigate |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Data and prospect databases | Coverage, contact access, speed to list | May start from names before fit, timing, or context are proven |
| Search and social tools | Fresh profiles, network context, visible activity | Often manual and rep-dependent |
| Enrichment and workflow builders | Flexible data assembly, custom workflows | Can require operational skill and ongoing maintenance |
| Sales engagement platforms | Repeatability, sequencing, activity tracking | Can scale generic touches if upstream inputs are weak |
| AI SDR / automation tools | Headcount leverage, automation, speed | Can move judgment too far from the human seller |
| Agencies or outsourced SDR | Labor, speed, external execution | Relationship capital and learning can sit outside the company |
| Internal manual process | Control, familiarity, no new vendor | Usually inconsistent, hard to inspect, and dependent on rep discipline |

These are prompts, not fixed truths. The actual downsides must come from the offer, buyer, competitors, and likely status quo.

## Coopetition Rule

A tool can be a competitor, complement, or input depending on how the buyer uses it.

- `Competitor`: the buyer uses it as the primary way to get the job done.
- `Complement`: the buyer can use it alongside the offer.
- `Input`: the offer depends on it or sends work into it.
- `Budget competitor`: the buyer could spend the same money there instead.

For the buyer-facing sheet, do not overstate competition. Say "works with" or "sits beside" when that is truer than "replaces."

## Strategy Matrix Lens

When useful, classify the offer's strategic posture:

| Strategy | Job performance | Price/cost | When it applies |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Differentiated | Better | More | Buyer is underserved and will pay for a better job outcome |
| Dominant | Better | Less | Rare; stronger outcome and lower cost |
| Disruptive | Worse or narrower at first | Less | Overserved or non-consuming buyers accept less to get access or lower cost |
| Discrete | Worse | More | Usually weak unless buyers have limited options |
| Sustaining | Similar or better for existing customers | Similar | Improves current category expectations without changing the basis of competition |

Use this for internal positioning only. The sales sheet should translate the conclusion into buyer language, not strategy jargon.
